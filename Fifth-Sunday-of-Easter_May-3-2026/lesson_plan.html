Sunday School Plan: Fifth Sunday of Easter

Christ is Risen! Christ is Risen, Indeed!
Scripture: Psalm 31:1-5, 15-16
Theme: God is Our Refuge, Rock, and Fortress

Master Materials List

  • Offering plate
  • A large bristol board (pre-divided into 4 sections using marker)
  • Markers or crayons for drawing
  • A phone or speaker to play music for the freeze dance game
  • Extra Sunday School Sheets and crayons/markers (for children who prefer to colour)

1. Introduction and Welcome

5 minutes: Weekend Check-In

Welcome everyone back! Ask the children how their week was and if anything fun or exciting happened. Let a few share. Then preview what is coming: "Today we are going to look at one of the oldest prayers ever written. It is called a psalm, and it was written by someone who was feeling really scared. They cried out to God and called him a rock, a fortress, a refuge. We are going to figure out what all of that means! But first, let's do our offering."

2. Offering and Prayer

1 minute: Offering

Collect the offering/donations while singing the money dropping song.

1 minute: Prayer over the Offering

"Risen Christ, we come to you today just like David did in this psalm, trusting that you are our safe place. Thank you for bringing us together. We offer this gift and we offer ourselves to you. Amen."

3. The Story

5 minutes: Interactive Story

The Story: (Read this narratively to the children using the iPad App. Encourage them to act it out with you as you read!)

"Have you ever felt really scared? Maybe in the dark, or when something felt wrong and you did not know what to do? A long, long time ago, someone wrote a prayer to God from exactly that feeling. That prayer became Psalm 31. [Can you make yourself as small as possible, like you are hiding?]

The writer cried out: "In you, O Lord, I seek refuge. Be a rock of refuge for me, a strong fortress to save me." A refuge is a place where you are completely safe, protected, and held. The writer believed that God could be that place. [Raise both hands up to the sky like you are calling out to God.]

A rock does not move. A fortress does not fall. When David called God a rock and a fortress, he was saying: God is the one thing that will never shift, never crumble, and never let me down. Inside God's protection, there is peace. [Freeze completely still. Plant your feet wide. Be the rock.]

Then the writer said the most beautiful thing: "Into your hand I commit my spirit." That means: God, I trust you completely with everything I am. And did you know? This is the same prayer Jesus prayed on the cross. And because of Easter, we know that those hands are safe hands. God holds us, and God does not let go." [Open both hands, palms up. Close your eyes. That is what trust looks like.]

4. Questions and Reflections

5 minutes: Ask the children the following questions:

  • The psalm is written by someone feeling really unsafe and scared. Have you ever felt that way? What did it feel like in your body when you were scared?
  • A refuge is a place of total safety and comfort. What would your perfect refuge look, sound, or feel like? (It could be a place, a person, a feeling.)
  • The writer calls God a "rock" and a "fortress." What word would YOU use to describe God if you were writing this psalm? (Let them answer freely. There are no wrong answers here.)

5. Bristol Board Drawing Activity

10 minutes: Drawing the Story Together

Transition: "David used really vivid picture words to describe God. Let's draw those pictures together on the bristol board!"

  • Section 1 (Someone Scared and Hiding): Have 1-2 children draw a person huddled and scared. Ask: "What does it feel like when you need somewhere safe to go?"
  • Section 2 (Crying Out to God): Have 1-2 children draw a person with arms raised, praying. Ask: "What did the writer ask God to be?"
  • Section 3 (The Rock and Fortress): Have 1-2 children draw a big rock or a fortress. Ask: "Why do you think the writer chose those words to describe God?"
  • Section 4 (Into Your Hands): Have the rest draw a pair of open hands with light coming from them. Ask: "What does it mean to commit your spirit into God's hands?"

6. Reading the Scripture

2 minutes: Psalm 31:1-5, 15-16

Transition: "Now that we have heard the story and drawn it, let's read the actual words of the psalm from the Bible!"

In you, O Lord, I seek refuge;
do not let me ever be put to shame;
in your righteousness deliver me.

Incline your ear to me;
rescue me speedily.
Be a rock of refuge for me,
a strong fortress to save me.

You are indeed my rock and my fortress;
for your name's sake lead me and guide me;
take me out of the net that is hidden for me,
for you are my refuge.

Into your hand I commit my spirit;
you have redeemed me, O Lord, faithful God.

My times are in your hand;
deliver me from the hand of my enemies and persecutors.
Let your face shine upon your servant;
save me in your steadfast love.

7. Game / Activity

20 minutes: "The Psalmist Says" Freeze Dance

(Note: Any children who don't want to participate can colour using the extra Sunday School sheets!)

Intro: "We are playing freeze dance again, but with brand new words from today's psalm! When I say 'The Psalmist says' before the word, you do the action. If I just say the word without it, you stay still. If you move, you are out for that round but back in the next one!"

The 5 Calls:

  • "Scared!" Make yourself as small as possible, crouch down, arms over your head.
  • "Refuge!" Wrap both arms around yourself in a big self-hug, close your eyes.
  • "Rock!" Freeze completely still. Feet planted wide. Arms out. Do not move a single muscle.
  • "Fortress!" Stand tall with hands on hips like a superhero guarding the gates.
  • "Hands!" Open both palms up toward the sky, eyes closed, soft smile. Trust.

How to Play (The Psalmist Says rules):

  1. Play upbeat music and let the children dance freely around the room.
  2. Stop the music. Only do the action if you say "The Psalmist says..." first. If you just say the word, they should stay still.
  3. Anyone who does the action without the prompt is out for that round and back in the next.
  4. Mix up speed, order, and whether you use the prompt to keep them guessing.
  5. At the end, ask: "Can anyone remember all five words from today's psalm?"

8. Wrap Up and Prayer

5 minutes: Wrap Up

  • Wrap up: Remind the children that this psalm has been prayed for thousands of years by people who were scared, who needed refuge, and who trusted God as their rock. We can pray it too. And because of Easter, we know that God's hands are exactly where we want to be.
  • Closing Prayer: "God, you are our rock. You are our fortress. You are our refuge. When we feel scared or small, remind us that we can say, just like David: into your hands I commit my spirit. Thank you that your hands are safe. In Jesus' name, Amen."
